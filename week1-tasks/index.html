What is Chess?

Chess is a board game for two players, called White and Black, each controlling an army of chess pieces. It is comprosed of 64 squares and 32 total pieces.

Meet the King

The king is considered priceless, as checkmate is reached when the king is cornered. The king can move in any direction but is limited to one square.

Meet the Queen

The queen is considered to have the highest numerical value of all the chess pieces due to its high functionality. The queen can move in all directions. It has a value of 9.

Meet the Rook

The rook can move vertically and horizontally on the board. It has a value of 5 and comes in handy during a move called 'castling'. Each player has two rooks.

Meet the Bishop

The bishop can move diagonally and has a value of 3. Each player starts of with two.

Meet the Knight

The knight moves in 'L' shaped patterns and is the only piece which can hop over other pieces. It also has a value of 3. Each player begins with two.

Meet the Pawn

Perhaps one of the least appreciated pieces, the pawn can only move one space forward after its first move, in which it can move up to two spaces. The pawn has a value of 1 and each player begins with 8 pawns.

Check

Check is a condition that occurs when a player's king is under threat of capture on the opponent's next turn

Checkmate

Checkmate is any game position in chess and other chess-like games in which a player's king is in check and there is no possible escape. Checkmating the opponent wins the game.

The Origins of Chess

Learn about how chess was started, how it's evolved, and why it's still being played 1500 years later

Precursors to chess originated in India. There, its early form in the 7th century CE was known as chaturaṅga (Sanskrit: चतुरङ्ग), which translates to "four divisions (of the military)": infantry, cavalry, elephantry, and chariotry. These forms are represented by the pieces that would evolve into the modern pawn, knight, bishop, and rook, respectively. Chess was introduced to Persia from India and became a part of the princely or courtly education of Persian nobility. Around 600 CE in Sassanid Persia, the name for the game became chatrang (Persian: چترنگ), which subsequently evolved to shatranj (Arabic: شطرنج; Persian: شترنج) after the conquest of Persia by the Rashidun Caliphate, due to the lack of native "ch" and "ng" sounds in the Arabic language. The rules were developed further during this time; players started calling "Shāh!" (Persian for "King!") when attacking the opponent's king, and "Shāh Māt!" (Persian for "the king is helpless" – see checkmate) when the king was attacked and could not escape from attack. These exclamations persisted in chess as it traveled to other lands.

Lectures

Chess lectures can be a fantastic way to elevate your game.

Topics: Lectures can cover a wide range, from basic rules (for beginners) to advanced strategy, tactics, and specific openings.

Learning: Listen to lectures by titled players or experienced coaches. They'll explain concepts, analyze famous games, and offer tips to improve your decision-making.

Benefits: Enhance your tactical vision, positional understanding, and opening repertoire. Lectures can also motivate you and expose you to new ideas.

Hands-on Sessions

Active Learning: Lectures provide knowledge, but hands-on sessions let you apply it. You'll play games, practice tactics, and receive feedback directly.

Develop Skills: Through active practice, you hone your tactical vision, strategic thinking, and calculation skills.

Get Feedback: An instructor can analyze your games, identify weaknesses, and suggest personalized improvements.

Formats: Options include group classes, private lessons, or chess clubs where you can play against others.

Tournaments

Chess tournaments are competitive events where players of all skill levels can test their abilities against each other. They come in various formats, time controls, and categories, offering something for everyone.

Improve your chess skills: Tournaments provide practical experience, allowing you to test your strategies and tactics against other players.

Develop sportsmanship: You learn to win gracefully and lose with dignity.

Make new friends: Tournaments are a great way to meet other chess enthusiasts and build a chess community.
